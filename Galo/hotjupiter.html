Hot Jupiters: Discovery, Types, and Current Data

Discovery of Hot Jupiters

Hot Jupiters were first discovered in 1995 when astronomers detected 51 Pegasi b, a gas giant orbiting extremely close to its parent star. This marked a major breakthrough in exoplanet research, as it challenged existing theories of planetary formation.

Types of Hot Jupiters

  • Classical Hot Jupiters: Gas giants that orbit very close to their parent star, resulting in high surface temperatures. These planets can have orbits as short as a few days.
  • Ultra-hot Jupiters: A sub-class of hot Jupiters that have extreme temperatures, often exceeding 2000°C, caused by their close proximity to their star.
  • Puffy Hot Jupiters: Hot Jupiters with lower density but large radii, often caused by the intense heat that causes their atmospheres to expand.

Current Data on Hot Jupiters

As of 2024, over 400 hot Jupiters have been discovered. Their orbits range from 0.01 to 0.1 AU (Astronomical Units) from their parent stars. Some of the most well-known hot Jupiters include:

  • WASP-121b: Known for its ultra-hot temperature and stratosphere.
  • HD 209458 b: Famous for having water vapor detected in its atmosphere.
  • Kepler-13A b: One of the hottest known hot Jupiters, with an atmospheric temperature of nearly 3000°C.
